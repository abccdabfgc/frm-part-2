Credit and Counterparty Risk

Credit Risk

Definition: The risk of economic loss from default or changes in ratings or other credit events

Basic Principles

  • A firms assets are financed by a combination of debt and equity capital of varying seniority and structures
  • Obligations can be secured or unsecured, recourse or non-recourse, and of varying priority

Frictions and Transaction Costs

  • Asymmetric information
  • Principal-agent problems
  • Risk shifting
  • Moral hazard
  • Adverse selection
  • Externalities
  • Collective action problems/coordination failures

Default and Recovery: Analytic Concepts

  • Default - failure to pay on a financial obligation
  • Bankruptcy - legal procedure in which an entity seeks temporary relief from its creditors
  • Probability of default
  • Exposure at default (EAD) - amount of money lender can potentially lose in a default
  • Loss given default (LGD) = EAD - Recovery amount
  • Expected loss (EL) = Probability of default Ã— LGD

Assessing Creditworthiness

  • Credit rating - an alphanumeric grade summarizing the creditworthiness of a security or corporate entity
  • Transition matrices - show estimated likelihood of a company with a given starting rating ending a period with a different rating
  • Internal ratings
  • Credit risk models
    • Reduced-form models - take default probability and LGD as inputs
    • Structural models - derive measures of credit risk from fundamental data
    • Factor models

Counterparty Risk

Definition: A special form of credit risk that arises whenever an investor requires another party to fulfill a contractual obligation and vice-versa

  • Netting - having one of the parties to a contract pay only the net amount, rather than exchaning principals

The Merton Model

  • Assumptions:
    • Value of assets follow geometric Brownian motion
    • Simple balance sheet
    • Limited liability/non-recourse
    • Strictly enforced contracts/no renegotiation
    • Assets of the firm are tradable
    • Default only possible at maturity
    • No interim cash flows
  • Default occurs if the value of the firm's assets are less than its debt repayment obligations

Credit Factor Models

Relate the risk of credit loss to fundamental economic variables

Credit Risk Measures

  • Credit losses have three components:
    1. Expected loss (EL)
    2. Unexpected loss (UL) - a quantile of the credit loss in excess of the expected loss
    3. Tail loss - losses beyond the UL
  • Jump-to-default risk - an estimate of the loss that would be realized if a position were to default instantly
